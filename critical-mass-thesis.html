/ Chapter 01

The Moment

Space is no longer a frontier in the exploratory sense. It is becoming infrastructure — and the interesting question is no longer whether we can reach orbit, but who will own its layers.

For sixty years, space was a state-funded program. For the last fifteen, it has been a venture experiment. The next ten years are something different: the construction of industrial infrastructure in low Earth orbit, in deep-space communications, in dual-use sensing, and in the manufacturing supply chain that supports all of it.

The hardest problems are no longer reaching orbit. They are sustaining commerce there, integrating it with terrestrial systems, and translating space-based intelligence into operating advantage on Earth. These are problems of execution, capital, and supply chain — the kind that reward technical depth, patience, and ownership.

For the first time, the economics of space rhyme with those of any other capital-intensive industry — durable, technical, and investable.

This is the operating environment Critical Mass was built for. We are not a vehicle for thematic exposure to "space." We are a concentrated, technically led capital partner for the companies actually building the infrastructure, intelligence, and industrial backbone of the space economy.

/ Chapter 02

Three Pillars.

Our mandate is concentrated and deliberate. We invest across three categories of the space economy where capital, technical depth, and timing converge.

/ 01 Infrastructure

The physical layer of the space economy — launch, in-space mobility, propulsion, satellite communications, ground systems, and on-orbit servicing. These are capital-intensive, hardware-defining companies whose advantage compounds with execution.

We believe value here accrues to operators with durable cost or capability advantages: launch teams who can hold cadence at price, propulsion systems that meet a meaningful customer specification, comms architectures with defensible network economics, servicing platforms with mission heritage. First movers with execution discipline accumulate moats that are difficult to replicate.

What we look for

Technical teams with launch, propulsion, or aerospace systems heritage. Defensible IP. A credible commercial or government pipeline at Series A. Capital efficiency with a path to recurring revenue or contracted backlog.

/ 02 Intelligence

The data and software layer — Earth observation, synthetic-aperture radar, signals intelligence, geospatial AI, autonomy, and the analytic platforms that turn raw orbital data into operational decisions for governments and enterprise.

Orbital data collection is becoming abundant; the value will accrue to companies that translate that abundance into decisions. The middle layer — tasking, fusion, geospatial AI, and decision platforms — is where defensible IP and durable customer relationships will compound. Pure pixel businesses face commoditization; intelligence businesses do not.

What we look for

Technical depth in machine learning, sensor fusion, or geospatial systems. Privileged access to proprietary data sources. Anchor commercial or government customers. Software margins underneath a hardware-aware product.

/ 03 Industrial Backbone

The supply chain that scales aerospace — advanced manufacturing, materials, sensors, power, components, and the dual-use industrial capacity that supports both commercial space and national defense.

Aerospace industrial capacity is structurally underbuilt. Commercial demand is growing into a supply chain that has not added capacity at the same rate, and defense industrial-base reorientation creates a parallel demand wave. Companies with proprietary manufacturing process, materials, or component IP can capture durable margins through this period.

What we look for

Process or materials IP that is difficult to replicate. Dual-use applicability. Capital-efficient growth path with manufacturing margins. Founder teams with operating depth in regulated industrial environments.

/ Chapter 03

Why Now.

Three structural changes have converged to make this decade categorically different from the last.

/ 01The cost curve has bent.

Cost-per-kilogram to low Earth orbit has fallen by an order of magnitude since 2010, and the curve has not flattened. Launch is no longer a hero project — it is a commodity service. This single shift unlocks entire categories of orbital business models that previously could not close their unit economics. The downstream effects ripple through every layer of the space economy.

/ 02Defense and commercial demand have converged.

Allied defense spend on space-based capabilities now exceeds $30 billion annually and is accelerating. For the first time in a generation, defense and commercial are converging on the same architectures — LEO communications, Earth observation, autonomy, dual-use sensing — and the same suppliers. This collapses the historical separation between commercial and defense pipelines and creates a structurally larger addressable market for technically credible operators.

/ 03The technical stack is finally ready.

The tools required to operate in space — modern AI, autonomous systems, advanced manufacturing, miniaturized sensors, software-defined radios — have matured in parallel with the cost-curve shift. A satellite today is closer in architecture to a modern data center than to a 1990s spacecraft. This compresses build cycles, lowers minimum viable scale, and rewards software-aware hardware teams.

Space is becoming the third major infrastructure layer of the modern economy — alongside terrestrial networks and computing. The companies building it are investable today.

/ Chapter 04

Capital Discipline.

Conviction without discipline is a thesis. Conviction with discipline is a fund.

Aerospace rewards a specific architecture of capital — concentrated, patient, and technically literate. Spray-and-pray underwrites adverse selection in this sector. Generalist money struggles to evaluate physics, supply chain, and regulatory exposure with the depth that real diligence requires. Operator-led specialist capital, deployed selectively and held long, has historically outperformed in technical sectors. We are built around that observation.

/ 01Concentration over coverage.

We hold a tight portfolio of companies in which we have meaningful ownership and a real seat at the table. We say no often so we can show up fully when we say yes. The cost of distraction in this sector is too high to spread thin.

/ 02Technical conviction over thematic exposure.

Diligence is led by partners who have built, operated, and capitalized companies in aerospace, defense, and technical infrastructure. We invest where we understand the physics, the supply chain, and the customer. We do not delegate technical judgment.

/ 03Patient follow-on capital.

Aerospace compounds slowly, then quickly. We hold for fundamentals, not market windows. We commit early, support meaningfully through Series B, and reserve capacity for selective continuation thereafter. Founders who partner with us know our timeline and our resolve.

/ 04Network as infrastructure.

Beyond capital, we provide structured access to primes, agencies, allied governments, and senior operators across propulsion, sensing, manufacturing, and software. Our network is treated as portfolio infrastructure — actively maintained, intentionally deployed, and measurably useful at the points in a company's life when it matters most.

/ Chapter 05

Boundaries.

A thesis is defined as much by what it excludes as by what it pursues. We are explicit about what we do not fund.

/ 01

Pure science without commercial path.

We are an investment partner, not a research grant. Companies whose value is defined entirely by future scientific breakthroughs without a credible commercial or contracted path are outside our mandate.

/ 02

Hype-led storefronts.

Strong narrative without underlying technical depth or unit economics is a category we have learned to recognize. We pass on companies whose primary asset is brand, regardless of valuation.

/ 03

Crewed and tourism-only platforms.

At this stage of the cycle, we view crewed spaceflight and orbital tourism as outside our mandate. The capital intensity, regulatory exposure, and timeline misalign with our hold framework.

/ 04

Single-program dependence.

Companies whose business model depends on a single subsidy, earmark, or contract vehicle carry concentration risk we will not underwrite. We back companies with real customers and real demand, not policy artifacts.

/ Chapter 06

The Decade Ahead.

The next ten years will resolve which companies become permanent fixtures of the space economy and which were tourists.

Our base case for the decade rests on four convictions. First, orbital commerce will mature into mainstream infrastructure: communications, observation, and timing services will move from specialty markets into default operating layers for terrestrial industries. Second, geospatial intelligence will be embedded in every major industry's decision stack — from agriculture and insurance to logistics, energy, and finance. Third, dual-use will become the default architecture, not the exception, with allied governments and commercial enterprises operating on shared technical foundations. Fourth, the winners will be technically deep, capital-efficient, and patient — the same qualities that have defined category-defining companies in every prior infrastructure buildout.

We do not need to be right about every company in the sector. We need to be right about a small number of them, and to back them with the conviction and capital they require.

That is the operating assumption of Critical Mass. Concentrated capital. Technical conviction. A ten-year horizon. And a partnership posture aligned with the founders building the layer of the modern economy that did not exist a decade ago.
